## Study Design

We designed Study 4 to test whether dorsal lightness and darkness in *Plethodon* salamanders vary predictably with geography and climate, with particular attention to contrasting expectations from Gloger's rule and the thermal melanism hypothesis (Clusella-Trullas et al., 2007; Delhey, 2019). The study also served as a methodological evaluation of an agent-guided optimization framework for ecological computer vision. Specifically, we asked whether a bounded large-language-model (LLM) loop could improve a phenotyping pipeline by iteratively refining extraction parameters over many short, repeatable experiments, while preserving an explicit record of the search trajectory (Karpathy, 2026; Schmidgall et al., 2025).

### Data source and preprocessing

All observations were obtained from iNaturalist, a large community-science platform that provides georeferenced, digitally vouchered biodiversity records (Di Cecco et al., 2021; iNaturalist contributors, 2026). We queried Research Grade observations for the genus *Plethodon* through the iNaturalist API and constructed a reproducible preprocessing workflow that included data acquisition, cleaning, and spatial binning. Cleaning steps removed observations lacking usable location data, filtered duplicate or low-quality records, standardized observation fields, and retained metadata required for downstream phenotyping and geographic analysis. Cleaned observations were then assigned to H3 hexagonal grid cells to support spatial aggregation and to provide a unit for estimating within-location variance in extracted color measurements.

From the cleaned observation table, we generated a photo manifest containing observation identifiers and the first associated image URL for each record with an available photograph. Images were downloaded locally for testing and at larger scale on a cloud virtual machine for full production runs. Downloaded images were stored by observation identifier to ensure a one-to-one mapping between phenotypic measurements and source records. This design allowed all downstream extraction, validation, and modeling steps to be reproduced from the same manifest and observation table.

### Agent-guided optimization of the color-extraction pipeline

The image-analysis workflow was framed as a constrained optimization problem. Starting from a baseline extraction configuration, an agent iteratively proposed one parameter change at a time, executed the revised extraction pipeline on a fixed validation subset, evaluated the result against a predefined score, and retained or rejected the change before moving to the next experiment. This approach was adapted from the `autoresearch` pattern, in which experimentation is treated as a logged sequence of bounded micro-experiments rather than a manually directed series of ad hoc adjustments (Karpathy, 2026).

The agent was permitted to modify only a restricted configuration dictionary rather than arbitrary source code. Tunable parameters included the fraction of the image retained in the crop, crop-placement strategy, color space used for brightness estimation, histogram-based brightness normalization, background-masking strategy, quality-control thresholds, and percentile trimming of extreme pixel values. This restriction served two purposes. First, it ensured that each experiment remained interpretable and directly comparable to prior experiments. Second, it reduced the risk of uncontrolled pipeline drift by confining the search to a biologically motivated and auditable parameter space.

For each experiment, the pipeline opened each image, converted it to RGB, optionally normalized brightness, extracted a crop from the dorsal region, computed image entropy as a quality-control measure, optionally applied a background mask, and then estimated dorsal brightness from the retained pixels. Brightness was calculated in one of several candidate color spaces, including HSV, CIE L\*a\*b\*, or luminance-weighted RGB. Images that failed brightness or entropy thresholds were excluded from scoring. Each experiment returned a set of extracted brightness values linked to observation coordinates and H3 cells.

### Evaluation metric and validation logic

We evaluated candidate extraction configurations using a composite score designed to reward geographic signal while penalizing local noise. For each experiment, we fit a simple regression of extracted brightness against latitude and computed the corresponding coefficient of determination ($R^2$). We then calculated the mean within-cell variance in brightness across H3 cells represented by at least three observations. The final score was defined as:

$$
\mathrm{Score} = R^2 - \lambda \times \mathrm{NormalizedWithinCellVariance},
$$

where $\lambda = 0.5$ in the present implementation. Under this formulation, higher scores indicate extraction settings that preserve broad geographic structure while minimizing variance attributable to photographic inconsistency within local areas. The use of a fixed validation subset and a fixed metric ensured that candidate configurations were evaluated under identical conditions throughout a run.

This metric was chosen because the central analytical challenge in community-science phenotyping is not simply to maximize contrast in the extracted values, but to identify measurements that are both geographically informative and robust to image heterogeneity. A pipeline that produces a strong latitudinal pattern only by amplifying photographic noise would be unsuitable for ecological inference. Conversely, a pipeline that aggressively suppresses variation but removes true biological structure would also be undesirable. The composite score therefore encoded a practical compromise between sensitivity to large-scale clines and stability within local spatial units.

### Cloud execution and full-scale extraction

To separate lightweight local preprocessing from compute-intensive image processing, we split the workflow across local and cloud environments. Local execution handled data acquisition, cleaning, grid assignment, statistical analysis, and figure generation on a 13-inch Apple MacBook Air (M3, 2024) with 16 GB memory running macOS Tahoe 26.3. Compute-intensive tasks, including large-scale image download, agent-guided parameter search, and final extraction, were run on Google Cloud Platform using a spot `n2-highmem-16` virtual machine (16 vCPUs, 128 GB RAM) in the `us-east1-b` zone, running Debian 12 with a 200 GB SSD boot disk provisioned at a spot rate of approximately $0.33/hr. The VM operated on a private subnet with outbound internet via Cloud NAT and was deployed using a self-contained startup script that required no interactive SSH access, executing the full pipeline autonomously: cloning the code repository, installing dependencies, downloading the photo corpus from Google Cloud Storage, running the optimization loop, performing final extraction, uploading results, and self-stopping on completion. The cloud environment evaluated many candidate configurations against the validation subset, recorded experiment-level outputs, and then applied the best-performing configuration to the full image set. Estimated total cloud cost for the full production run was approximately $3.50, comprising photo download (~$0.59), agent-guided optimization (~$1.65), final extraction (~$0.83), and disk provisioning (~$0.34). This arrangement reduced the time cost of large-scale phenotyping while preserving a reproducible local analysis environment for downstream statistics and figure generation.

All experiments were logged with the full parameter configuration and summary statistics, including the composite score, regression fit, within-cell variance, number of extracted observations, and the proportion of validation images passing quality control. Logging each micro-experiment created an audit trail of the optimization process and made it possible to reconstruct how the final extraction settings were obtained. In this respect, the methodological contribution of the study lies not only in the final parameter set, but also in the explicit formalization of phenotyping optimization as a constrained, reproducible search process.

### Downstream analysis

After parameter optimization, the final extraction configuration was applied to all available photos that passed download and quality-control checks. Extracted color measurements were merged back to the cleaned observation table, yielding a dataset that linked dorsal brightness to species identity, coordinates, year, and H3 cell. Analyses were structured into three tiers:

**Primary analysis (within-species, *P. cinereus*)**: Because interspecific brightness differences confound geographic analysis when species are pooled, the primary test of ecogeographic hypotheses used only *Plethodon cinereus* observations (~69% of the dataset). Holding species identity constant, we regressed individual-level dorsal brightness against latitude and longitude, and repeated the analysis at the H3 cell level to reduce pseudoreplication. A positive latitude coefficient (brighter northward) would support Gloger's rule; a negative coefficient (darker northward) would support the thermal melanism hypothesis.

**Secondary analysis (within-species, other species)**: We repeated the latitude–brightness regression for each additional species with at least 50 observations (*P. vehiculum*, *P. albagula*, *P. dorsalis*, *P. serratus*, and others). Concordance of slope direction across species would indicate a genus-wide ecogeographic pattern rather than a species-specific one. For members of the *P. glutinosus* species complex — a group of ~16 cryptic species split by Highton et al. (1989) that are frequently confused on iNaturalist — we applied a stricter identification filter requiring at least three agreeing community identifications before including an observation in within-species regression. Results for glutinosus complex members were flagged and interpreted with caution; if slope directions appeared driven by shifting species composition rather than within-species variation, those species were excluded from the secondary summary.

**Exploratory analysis (cross-species comparison)**: We tested whether species mean brightness correlated with species mean latitude across all species with at least 10 observations, and used a Kruskal–Wallis test to confirm that brightness distributions differed among species. This analysis is inherently confounded by phylogenetic non-independence and was treated as hypothesis-generating.

Taken together, this study design paired a substantive ecological question with a deliberately constrained agentic workflow. Biologically, the study tested whether dorsal coloration in *Plethodon* exhibits geographic structure consistent with classic ecogeographic expectations. Methodologically, it evaluated whether an LLM-guided loop can serve as a practical tool for parameter search in ecological image analysis by combining explicit objectives, bounded intervention, cloud-based execution, and experiment-level logging.
