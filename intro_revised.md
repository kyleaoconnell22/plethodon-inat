## Introduction

Much of modern computational research consists of repeated local decisions: selecting preprocessing rules, adjusting thresholds, varying model settings, rerunning analyses, inspecting diagnostics, and deciding which change to evaluate next. These steps are individually modest, but collectively they determine the speed, cost, and reproducibility of empirical work. Recent advances in large language models (LLMs) suggest that some of this iterative labor can be formalized as a bounded research loop in which an agent proposes a small change, executes a short experiment, evaluates the outcome against an explicit metric, and retains or discards the result before proceeding to the next step (Zhang et al., 2025; Schmidgall et al., 2025).

This use of agents is narrower than open-ended "AI scientist" narratives and, for that reason, may be more immediately practical. Rather than replacing scientific judgment, the agent operates inside a search space defined by the researcher, under a fixed compute budget and an explicit scoring rule. Karpathy's `autoresearch` provides a concise demonstration of this pattern, treating experimentation itself as a loop: modify a constrained part of the pipeline, run a time-bounded trial, score the result, preserve improvements, and log the trajectory of the search (Karpathy, 2026). The broader implication is that research tasks organized around many auditable micro-experiments may be amenable to the same approach even when the downstream application is not machine learning per se.

This framework is especially attractive for computational studies that couple ideation, analysis, and cloud execution. In such settings, agents can assist with literature-grounded planning, parameter search, remote job orchestration, and structured reporting of experimental outcomes. The potential advantage is not autonomy in the strongest sense, but a reduction in the overhead of disciplined iteration, together with a more explicit record of which alternatives were tried, rejected, or retained. For scientific workflows that are repetitive, evaluable, and straightforward to log, agent-guided iteration may therefore offer a useful operational model.

We evaluate this idea in an ecological computer-vision setting using open community-science data. Specifically, we use publicly available iNaturalist observations to test ecogeographic hypotheses about dorsal coloration in woodland salamanders of the genus *Plethodon*. iNaturalist has become a major source of digitally vouchered biodiversity records and now supports large-scale research across taxa and regions, while also introducing well-known challenges related to observer behavior, sampling bias, and image heterogeneity (Di Cecco et al., 2021; iNaturalist contributors, 2026). For phenotype-focused analyses, these tradeoffs are particularly consequential: the platform provides extensive geographic coverage and photographic evidence, but the images vary substantially in lighting, framing, background, and quality. This combination makes iNaturalist a useful test bed for agent-guided analytical iteration, because extracting biological signal from the images depends on a long sequence of parameterized, repeatable image-processing decisions.

Our biological case study concerns dorsal pigmentation in *Plethodon*, one of the most intensively studied salamander genera in North America. *Plethodon* has long served as a model system for ecological and evolutionary research, including classic work on competition, elevational segregation, and geographic variation (Hairston, 1951; Jaeger, 1971; Highton, 1962). The genus is especially well suited to ecogeographic analysis because many species occupy strong climatic gradients across eastern North America, including substantial variation in latitude, temperature, moisture, and elevation. These gradients motivate a long-standing question in evolutionary ecology: whether broad-scale color variation is more consistent with Gloger's rule, which predicts darker pigmentation in warm, humid environments, or with the thermal melanism hypothesis, which predicts darker pigmentation in colder environments where increased absorptivity may improve thermoregulation (Clusella-Trullas et al., 2007; Delhey, 2019). Because these hypotheses can imply opposing spatial patterns, the direction of a pigmentation cline is itself informative.

Within *Plethodon*, the eastern red-backed salamander, *P. cinereus*, provides an especially tractable anchor for this analysis. This species is widespread, abundant, ecologically important, and well known for its striped red-backed and unstriped lead-backed morphs, whose frequencies vary geographically and have long been discussed in relation to climate and selection (Lotter and Scott, 1977; Kraemer et al., 2012; Hantak et al., 2021; Moore and Ouellet, 2025). The existence of this conspicuous and biologically meaningful variation makes *Plethodon* an ideal proof-of-concept system for image-based phenotyping. If a computer-vision pipeline is recovering ecologically relevant dorsal brightness information, it should detect structured variation across morphs, populations, and environmental gradients rather than merely recapitulating noise introduced by cameras, backgrounds, and field conditions.

In this system, the competing hypotheses are well defined. Under Gloger's rule, *Plethodon* populations occupying warmer and wetter environments are expected to exhibit darker dorsal pigmentation, implying darker phenotypes at lower latitudes and in more mesic settings (Delhey, 2019). Under the thermal melanism hypothesis, darker pigmentation is instead expected in colder environments because increased absorptivity can facilitate heat gain in ectotherms, implying darker phenotypes at higher latitudes, higher elevations, or both (Clusella-Trullas et al., 2007). In polymorphic taxa such as *P. cinereus*, these predictions may also be reflected in shifts in morph frequencies across climatic gradients (Lotter and Scott, 1977; Hantak et al., 2021). The direction and strength of any recovered brightness cline are therefore directly relevant to inference about the dominant selective pressures acting on color variation in this system.

We therefore use *Plethodon* as a case study for an agent-guided scientific workflow. In our framework, the agent does not generate unconstrained analyses; instead, it operates inside a narrow optimization loop that proposes bounded changes to a color-extraction pipeline, evaluates those changes against a predefined metric, and iterates across cloud-based micro-experiments. The biological objective is to determine whether dorsal lightness and darkness vary predictably with geography and climate in ways more consistent with Gloger's rule, thermal melanism, or a more complex combination of selective pressures. The methodological objective is to assess whether LLM-guided agent loops can serve as a practical tool for parameter search, pipeline refinement, and reproducible exploratory analysis over open data. By combining community-science imagery, computer vision, and an explicit agentic optimization framework, this study tests both the ecological hypothesis and the utility of bounded agentic iteration as a scientific method.
